<Section index={1} title="1833, Dublin">

<PhysicistLink slug="william-rowan-hamilton">William Rowan Hamilton</PhysicistLink> was obsessed, for most of the 1820s, with a question that sounded half-metaphysical: why does light, in going from A to B, take the path it takes? He had built, by 1828, a complete geometric theory of optics in which every ray could be read off from the gradient of a single scalar function. The function told you, at every point, the shortest time to reach it. The rays were the lines along which that function changed fastest.

Then in 1833 he did a thing that nobody had quite anticipated. He took the same mathematical machinery and pointed it at mechanics. Light rays and planet orbits, he showed, were the same type of object — extremals of a variational principle, perpendicular to level sets of a scalar, generated by a function he simply called *H*.

The reformulation that came out of that analogy is used, today, literally by every physicist who simulates a molecule, a spacecraft trajectory, or the interior of a neutron star. Quantum mechanics took it over wholesale: the Schrödinger equation is *i ℏ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ*, where Ĥ is the operator version of Hamilton's *H*. Nothing in twentieth-century physics makes sense without this chapter.

The chapter, fortunately, is short. It is basically one swap — position and velocity go in, position and momentum come out — and everything else follows.

</Section>

<Section index={2} title="One swap: Legendre transform">

The <PhysicistLink slug="joseph-louis-lagrange">Lagrangian</PhysicistLink> formulation we met in the last topic describes a system by a single function *L(q, q̇, t)* — kinetic minus potential energy — with the equations of motion derived by extremising the action ∫L dt. The natural variables are position *q* and velocity *q̇*.

Hamilton's move is to replace *q̇* with a new variable — the **<Term slug="conjugate-momentum">conjugate momentum</Term>** — defined as the derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to velocity:

<EquationBlock id="EQ.01">
  <span className="font-mono text-lg">p = ∂L/∂q̇</span>
</EquationBlock>

For a plain Newtonian particle with *L = ½m·q̇² − V(q)*, that definition gives *p = m·q̇* — the familiar momentum. For a particle in a magnetic field it gives something richer (*p = m·q̇ + qA*). For a bead on a rotating wire, something richer still. The definition is general; the special case you already know is just the simplest case.

Now perform the swap. Define the **<Term slug="hamiltonian">Hamiltonian</Term>** as

<EquationBlock id="EQ.02">
  <span className="font-mono text-lg">H(q, p, t) = p · q̇ − L(q, q̇, t)</span>
</EquationBlock>

with q̇ eliminated in favour of *p* via the definition above. This is a **Legendre transform** — the same algebraic trick that turns internal energy into free energy in thermodynamics. It swaps an independent variable for its conjugate without losing any information.

<Callout variant="math">
The Legendre transform is symmetric: transforming *H* back by the same recipe returns *L*. Lagrangian and Hamiltonian are two dialects of the same physics, related by a single algebraic rotation. Physicists keep both around because each dialect makes different things easy.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section index={3} title="Hamilton's equations">

Plug the definition of *H* into the Euler-Lagrange equations and two lines of algebra later you have a pair of equations that is the whole dynamical content of classical mechanics:

<EquationBlock id="EQ.03">
  <span className="font-mono text-lg">q̇ = ∂H/∂p   ,   ṗ = −∂H/∂q</span>
</EquationBlock>

These are **<Term slug="hamiltons-equations">Hamilton's equations</Term>**. Read them. They are symmetric in *q* and *p*, with a single sign keeping the system from collapsing. They are **first-order** — one derivative each — where the Lagrangian and Newtonian formulations are second-order. A 2N-dimensional state space (N positions, N momenta) evolves as a first-order flow.

That geometry is the point. At each instant the system's state is a single point *(q, p)*. Hamilton's equations hand you a vector field on that state space. The trajectory is just the integral curve of the field. No ambiguity, no integration constants hiding in a corner — you specify where the point starts, and the field tells it where to go next.

For a single particle in a potential *V(q)*, the Hamiltonian is

<EquationBlock id="EQ.04">
  <span className="font-mono text-lg">H(q, p) = p² / 2m  +  V(q)</span>
</EquationBlock>

and Hamilton's equations give q̇ = p/m (which says *p = m·q̇*, momentum) and ṗ = −∂V/∂q (which says ṗ = F, Newton's second law). Newton falls out as the simplest case, same as before — but now position and momentum live on equal footing.

</Section>

<Section index={4} title="H is the energy">

For any system whose Lagrangian does not explicitly depend on time — which is to say, any isolated system, any system obeying time-translation symmetry — the Hamiltonian equals the total energy:

<EquationBlock id="EQ.05">
  <span className="font-mono text-lg">H = T + V</span>
</EquationBlock>

Kinetic plus potential, on the nose. The plus sign matters: the Lagrangian is *T − V*, the Hamiltonian is *T + V*. Subtract to get equations of motion; add to get the conserved quantity.

This is not a coincidence. It is <PhysicistLink slug="emmy-noether">Noether's theorem</PhysicistLink> in its cleanest garb. If *H* does not explicitly depend on *t*, then along any trajectory *dH/dt = 0* — the energy is constant. Chain-rule this by hand and you'll see the cancellation: Hamilton's two equations are precisely antisymmetric in the one way that makes *H* itself invariant.

<Callout variant="intuition">
You have now met energy conservation three times: as bookkeeping in FIG.04, as a symmetry consequence in FIG.08, and as a derivative identity in FIG.30. The three statements are the same fact viewed from three different abstractions. Each time we reformulate mechanics, energy conservation gets easier to see — until in the Hamiltonian picture it is basically typography.
</Callout>

For the simple pendulum, *H = p²/(2mL²) − mgL·cos θ*, where *p = mL²·θ̇* is the angular momentum conjugate to θ. Fix a value of *H* and you have an equation relating θ and *p*. Each value of *H* traces out a curve in the *(θ, p)* plane. The picture that comes out is exactly the phase portrait we met back in FIG.01:

<SceneCard caption="FIG.30a — the pendulum in (θ, p)" className="w-full">
  <PhasePortrait theta0={0.4} length={1.2} />
</SceneCard>

Small oscillations trace ellipses around the origin. Larger amplitudes trace fatter, deformed ellipses. Go past the top of the swing and closed loops open into flowing curves of rotation. The boundary between the two regimes — the separatrix — is the set of trajectories at exactly *H = mgL*, just barely over the top. Every mechanical system's qualitative behaviour is in its phase portrait, and the phase portrait is just the level sets of *H*.

</Section>

<Section index={5} title="Poisson brackets">

Any smooth function *f(q, p, t)* on phase space has a time derivative along a Hamiltonian trajectory. Chain rule it, plug in Hamilton's equations, and you get a formula that deserves its own name. Define the **<Term slug="poisson-bracket">Poisson bracket</Term>** of two functions *f* and *g* as

<EquationBlock id="EQ.06">
  <span className="font-mono text-base">{"{f, g} = ∑ᵢ ( ∂f/∂qᵢ · ∂g/∂pᵢ  −  ∂f/∂pᵢ · ∂g/∂qᵢ )"}</span>
</EquationBlock>

Then the time evolution of any observable is

<EquationBlock id="EQ.07">
  <span className="font-mono text-base">{"df/dt  =  {f, H}  +  ∂f/∂t"}</span>
</EquationBlock>

Read that carefully. The Hamiltonian, bracketed against any quantity you care about, tells you how that quantity changes in time. It is the generator of time evolution — the thing that moves everything.

<PhysicistLink slug="simeon-denis-poisson">Poisson</PhysicistLink> introduced this bracket in 1809, before Hamilton's reformulation existed, originally as a calculational tool in celestial mechanics. Hamilton picked it up and made it structural. The brackets satisfy three properties that any algebraist would flag as important: antisymmetry {"{f, g} = −{g, f}"}, linearity in each slot, and a thing called the Jacobi identity {"{f, {g, h}} + {g, {h, f}} + {h, {f, g}} = 0"}.

These three properties are the definition of a *Lie algebra*. Poisson was computing in one a century before the structure had a name.

<Callout variant="intuition">
Here is the payoff, and it is the sort of fact that physics students remember for the rest of their lives. Ninety-one years after Poisson, quantum mechanics was being invented in Göttingen and Copenhagen. Paul Dirac noticed that if you take every classical observable and turn it into an operator, and replace every Poisson bracket with *(1/iℏ)* times a commutator of the corresponding operators, **Hamilton's equations become Heisenberg's equations**. The structure is identical. Quantum mechanics is classical Hamiltonian mechanics where the Poisson bracket was quietly replaced by an operator commutator. That is not a metaphor or a loose analogy. It is the literal correspondence Dirac built the theory from in 1925.
</Callout>

Classical physicists had the algebra of quantum mechanics in their hands for a century without realising it. Every calculation with Poisson brackets done in the 1800s was, up to an ℏ-sized correction, a quantum calculation.

</Section>

<Section index={6} title="Symplectic structure and Liouville's theorem">

Hamilton's equations do something geometrically special. Take any small region in phase space — a cloud of possible initial conditions, each a point *(q, p)* — and let every point in the cloud evolve under Hamilton's equations. The shape of the cloud will distort wildly over time. Its **volume**, however, stays exactly the same forever.

This is **<Term slug="liouvilles-theorem">Liouville's theorem</Term>**, proved by <PhysicistLink slug="joseph-liouville">Joseph Liouville</PhysicistLink> in 1838. It follows from Hamilton's equations in one paragraph: the phase-space velocity field *(q̇, ṗ) = (∂H/∂p, −∂H/∂q)* is divergence-free — ∂q̇/∂q + ∂ṗ/∂p = ∂²H/∂q∂p − ∂²H/∂p∂q = 0 — so the flow it generates preserves volume. The cross-partials commute and that simple fact is the whole proof.

The preserved object is actually richer than volume. What Hamiltonian flow conserves is a **<Term slug="symplectic">symplectic</Term>** 2-form — an oriented area element that generalises volume and carries the antisymmetry of the Poisson bracket. "Symplectic" (from Greek *symplektikos*, "woven together") is the geometric name for phase space done right: positions and momenta intertwined by a single piece of structure.

<Callout variant="math">
Conservative systems preserve phase-space volume. Dissipative systems, which have friction or drag pulling trajectories toward attractors, always *lose* volume. The distinction between "energy-conserving" and "dissipative" becomes geometric: the first kind of system has a symplectic flow, the second kind shrinks phase-space volume over time. You can spot a Hamiltonian system from its phase portrait alone.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section index={7} title="Symplectic integrators">

The theorem is not just pretty. It has a consequence that shows up the next time you open a numerical simulation.

Suppose you want to simulate the solar system on a computer. The simplest way is the forward-Euler method: at each step, take the current state, compute the forces, and push *q* and *p* forward by dt times their derivatives. It is the thing any programmer invents in a first-year class.

Forward Euler is not symplectic. It does not preserve phase-space volume. Over many integration steps, the volume grows — and for a bound orbit, that corresponds to the planet spiralling outward, gaining energy it physically should not have.

There is an alternative, barely more complex, called **leapfrog** (or Störmer-Verlet). It updates *p* on half-steps and *q* on full steps, in a pattern that happens to preserve the symplectic 2-form exactly. Leapfrog is first-order simple to code, second-order accurate in dt, and conserves *H* up to bounded oscillations *for any step size, forever*. Watch:

</SceneCard>

Same starting conditions. Same potential. Same step size. The left panel uses forward Euler; its orbit drifts outward orbit after orbit. The right panel uses leapfrog; its orbit traces the same ellipse for as long as you are willing to watch. The readout in each corner shows *ΔH*, the drift from the initial energy — bounded on the right, creeping upward on the left.

This is not a contrived example. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory's DE ephemerides — the files that tell every spacecraft where every planet will be in 2035 — are computed with symplectic integrators because no non-symplectic scheme stays accurate over the decades of wall-clock integration they require. Your weather model can get away with forward Euler on a one-week forecast. A hundred-million-year solar-system simulation cannot.

<Callout variant="intuition">
The abstract geometric theorem Liouville proved in 1838 — *Hamiltonian flow preserves phase-space volume* — is the reason a modern interplanetary mission plan does not silently go wrong somewhere in deep space. Writing code that respects the symplectic structure costs about three extra lines compared to the naive version. Not writing it would have ended the Voyager program at Neptune.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section index={8} title="What's next">

We have a whole new arena. Every classical system — one particle or a trillion — sits as a single point in a space where positions and momenta are drawn in independent directions. Hamilton's equations carve out a flow on that space. Symmetries carve out conserved slices. Poisson brackets turn into commutators, and the classical world turns into the quantum one.

That space has a name, and it deserves its own page.
