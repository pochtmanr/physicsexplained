<Section index={1} title="The pattern">

Three conservation laws, three topics. Energy is conserved. Momentum is conserved. Angular momentum is conserved. Each law has been independently useful — we used energy conservation to pin down the speed of a falling brick, momentum conservation to solve collisions, angular-momentum conservation to explain spinning skaters.

Stop and notice the pattern. Why exactly three of them? Why these three? Why not also m²·v, or m·v³, or the quantity m·x² — why do those not admit conservation laws? There are infinitely many things you could imagine being conserved, and the universe picks three. Is there a rule?

There is. It is the most beautiful theorem in classical physics, proved by a single mathematician in 1918, and it answers the question at one stroke. **For every continuous symmetry of a physical system, there is a conserved quantity.** That is it. The three laws we've built are exactly the three symmetries of space and time that the universe turns out to have, and no others. Were the universe to have a fourth continuous symmetry, it would have a fourth conservation law. It doesn't, and it doesn't.

The mathematician was <PhysicistLink slug="emmy-noether">Emmy Noether</PhysicistLink>. The theorem is her name. The rest of this topic is what it means and why.

</Section>

<Section index={2} title="What is a symmetry?">

The word is used loosely in everyday speech. In physics it has a precise meaning: a system has a **<Term slug="symmetry">symmetry</Term>** under some transformation if applying the transformation changes nothing about the physics. The laws of motion of the transformed system are identical to the laws of motion of the original system. An experiment run before the transformation and an experiment run after will produce indistinguishable results.

Three candidate transformations of the physical world:

1. **Time translation.** If I run an experiment today, and an identical experiment tomorrow, they produce identical results. The laws of physics don't change with time. Nothing special about Wednesday versus Thursday. The system has a symmetry under t → t + Δt, for any Δt.

2. **Space translation.** If I run an experiment in Dortmund, and an identical experiment in Berlin, they produce identical results. Nothing special about one point in space over another — gravity, electromagnetism, pendulums, springs all behave the same way. Symmetry under r → r + Δr.

3. **Rotation.** If I orient my apparatus pointing east, and you orient yours pointing north, we get identical physics. Space has no preferred direction. Symmetry under r → R·r, for any rotation R.

There are others (reflections, parity, time reversal), but these three are the fundamental *continuous* symmetries — meaning the transformation can be applied by any tiny amount, not just in discrete jumps. Continuity is what Noether's theorem needs.

<Section index={3} title="Noether's theorem, stated">

Noether's theorem is a statement in Lagrangian mechanics. (We will do Lagrangians properly in FIG.29; for now, take it that for every mechanical system you can write a single function L of positions and velocities whose behaviour over time encodes all the equations of motion. The function is called the **<Term slug="lagrangian">Lagrangian</Term>**, usually defined as L = KE − PE.)

Noether proved, in its classical form:

> If the Lagrangian of a system is invariant under a continuous transformation depending on a parameter ε, then there is a quantity Q that does not change with time — a conserved quantity — whose form can be read directly off the transformation.

"Invariant" means: applying the transformation makes no difference to L. "Continuous" means the transformation can be infinitesimal. Q is the generator of the transformation.

The three classical cases, filled in:

- **Time-translation symmetry** (L doesn't depend on t explicitly) → Q = energy
- **Space-translation symmetry** (L doesn't depend on x explicitly) → Q = momentum
- **Rotation symmetry** (L is invariant under rotations) → Q = angular momentum

Every conservation law we met in FIG.05, FIG.06, FIG.07 is a specific instance of this theorem. Three topics of careful construction turned out to be three corollaries of one sentence.

  <span className="font-mono text-base">symmetry (continuous)    ⇔    conserved quantity</span>
</EquationBlock>

Read the arrow both ways. A symmetry implies a conserved quantity — the forward direction is what Noether proved. But the converse also holds: observing a conserved quantity is direct evidence that the system has the corresponding symmetry. When we discover a new conservation law, we have discovered a new symmetry of the universe. When a conservation law is broken, we have discovered that a symmetry we thought existed doesn't — or is broken by some new physics.

</Section>

<Section index={4} title="Beyond mechanics">

The theorem's reach is enormous. In classical mechanics it unifies our three conservation laws. In electromagnetism and quantum field theory — formalisms Noether herself did not live long enough to see fully developed — it unifies every conservation law in all of modern physics.

- **Conservation of electric charge** comes from the symmetry of quantum wave functions under a continuous phase rotation ψ → e^(iα)·ψ. The phase of a wave function is physically unobservable; Noether tells us that the conserved consequence of this fact is charge.

- **Conservation of color charge** in quantum chromodynamics comes from a more complicated symmetry (the gauge group SU(3)), but it too is a Noether consequence of an unobservable phase.

- **Conservation of lepton number and baryon number** come from separate global phase symmetries.

- In general relativity, the absence of a single well-defined energy-momentum tensor for the gravitational field is a consequence of the theory's diffeomorphism invariance being a *local* symmetry rather than a rigid global one — Noether's theorem behaves differently for local gauge symmetries, and her *second* theorem (less famous but equally deep) handles those cases. <PhysicistLink slug="albert-einstein">Einstein</PhysicistLink> specifically consulted Noether about this subtlety when formulating general relativity — and <PhysicistLink slug="david-hilbert">Hilbert</PhysicistLink> and <PhysicistLink slug="felix-klein">Klein</PhysicistLink>, two of the foremost mathematicians of the time, credited her with solving it.

This is why Noether is frequently called the most important woman in the history of mathematics. Her 1918 theorem is the skeleton key to all of modern physics. Anywhere a quantity is conserved, somewhere a symmetry is hiding. Anywhere a symmetry is broken, somewhere a conservation law fails — and that failure is how new physics gets discovered.

</Section>

<Section index={5} title="Why the universe has just these three">

We can now answer the question from §1. Why exactly three classical conservation laws?

Because the universe has exactly three continuous symmetries of the simplest kind: time has one continuous dimension, and is invariant under sliding along it (one symmetry); space has three continuous dimensions, and is invariant under sliding along any of them (three symmetries, but by convention bundled into one "momentum" vector); space is invariant under three independent axes of rotation (one rotation symmetry, bundled into an "angular momentum" vector). Lump them as "time-shifts, space-shifts, rotations" and you get three Noether-charges: energy (scalar), momentum (3-vector), angular momentum (3-vector).

No others are fundamental in non-relativistic classical mechanics. Galilean boosts — moving to a uniformly moving frame — are a symmetry of Newtonian physics but lead not to a new conserved quantity of the sort we want; they give the weaker statement that the motion of the center of mass is uniform, which is already implicit in momentum conservation. In relativity, Galilean boosts are replaced by Lorentz boosts, and those yield no scalar conserved charge in the usual sense either — instead they rotate energy and momentum into each other, which is why in relativity they are a single 4-vector.

That is the full list for classical mechanics. Three symmetries, three conservation laws, no more, no less. Noether settles the count at one stroke. Whatever other conservation laws you discover in nature — of charge, of isospin, of baryon number — are reports on deeper, non-spatial symmetries whose existence is Noether-encoded in the structure of the theory.

</Section>

<Section index={6} title="Emmy Noether">

Amalie Emmy Noether was born in Erlangen in 1882, the daughter of the mathematician Max Noether. She wanted to study mathematics at a time when German universities did not admit women. She audited lectures at Erlangen from 1900 to 1903 — only two women were permitted in a class of nearly a thousand men — and managed to formally matriculate when rules briefly changed. She took her doctorate in 1907 on a topic in invariant theory that she later described as "crap".

From 1908 to 1915 she worked as an unpaid assistant to her father and produced original mathematics no one paid her to do. In 1915 Hilbert and Klein, recognising her extraordinary talent, invited her to Göttingen — then the world's mathematical capital — to help them with the mathematical formulation of general relativity. The university refused to let her hold a paid teaching position. Hilbert's famous response: *"I do not see that the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission as a Privatdozent. After all, the Senate is not a bathhouse."* She lectured under Hilbert's name for years.

In 1918 she proved the theorem that is her memorial. Einstein wrote, the following year, to Hilbert: *"Yesterday I received from Miss Noether a very interesting paper on invariance. I am impressed that one can handle these things from such a general point of view. It would not have hurt had the old guard at Göttingen picked up some instruction from her."*

She became a formal (but still under-paid) Privatdozent in 1919 and a tenured professor in 1922 — the first woman to hold a full mathematics chair in Germany. She founded modern abstract algebra, and the structures she introduced — Noetherian rings, Noetherian modules — still bear her name and form the backbone of commutative algebra. When the Nazis expelled Jews from German universities in 1933 she emigrated to the United States and took a position at Bryn Mawr. She died in 1935, aged 53, from complications following an ovarian-cyst operation.

Her theorem alone would have been a career's worth of work. She did it in her spare time. The rest of her career she spent founding the structural approach to modern algebra that shaped twentieth-century mathematics. Einstein, who did not use the word lightly, called her "the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced since the higher education of women began."

</Section>

<Section index={7} title="Forward">

This is the natural close of the conservation-laws module. We built three ledgers (FIG.05–07), then gave them a single explanation (FIG.08). A set of apparently independent empirical laws have turned into three facets of one theorem.

What next? Module 3 begins the deeper study of rotation. We've been throwing around moment of inertia, angular momentum, and torque as rotational analogues of mass, momentum, and force — but we haven't looked at them carefully. Rotational motion has its own surprises: a hollow sphere rolls slower than a solid one, a spinning top stands up rather than falls, and the Earth itself wobbles on its axis every 433 days in a way that was not predicted until <PhysicistLink slug="leonhard-euler">Euler</PhysicistLink> worked out rigid-body dynamics in the 1750s. Angular momentum is not just translation's rotational cousin; it has its own character, and the next four topics are about it.

One more thing to carry forward from Noether. When we reach FIG.28 and begin the principle of least action, we will introduce the Lagrangian formally, and Noether's theorem will appear again — this time as a direct geometric consequence of action-invariance. By the time we close this branch you will have met the theorem three times: as a pattern observed, as a theorem stated, and as a principle built into the deepest formulation of mechanics we know. That progression — phenomenon, law, structure — is how physics keeps going.
