New physicists needed (check existing 92 PHYSICISTS first; only add missing):
— §09.3 fresnel-equations: Augustin-Jean Fresnel (1788–1827). French engineer-turned-physicist. Fresnel formulae 1818–1823; lighthouse lens design (the "Fresnel lens" that made every lighthouse in the world visible at sea for the next hundred years); wave theory of light against Laplace's particle faction at the French Academy. Died of tuberculosis at 39; the Copley Medal arrived weeks before his death. Perfect Kurzgesagt hook.
— §09.4 total-internal-reflection (referenced but primary use), §09.6 geometric-optics, §09.10 waveguides: Willebrord Snel van Royen (1580–1626), latinised as Snellius. Dutch astronomer at Leiden; 1621 derived the sine law of refraction but never published; Descartes independently re-derived it in 1637 and published, so for 200 years the law was "Descartes's law" in France. Died at 46 of fever. First to propose triangulation as a geodetic method — measured the Earth's radius by surveying a chain of triangles from Alkmaar to Bergen op Zoom.
— §09.9 polarization-phenomena: David Brewster (1781–1868). Scottish physicist and theologian. Discovered Brewster's angle 1815 by systematic experimentation across a huge number of materials. Edited the Edinburgh Encyclopaedia; invented the kaleidoscope (patented 1817 — instantly stolen by London hawkers; Brewster lost the manufacturing rights within months). Founded the BAAS (British Association for the Advancement of Science). President of the Royal Society of Edinburgh twice.
— §09.7 interference + §09.8 diffraction background + §09.4 spectroscopy bridge: Joseph von Fraunhofer (1787–1826). Bavarian, orphaned at 11, apprenticed to a glassmaker; worked in terrible conditions until the roof collapsed on him in 1801 (he survived; the prince elector personally visited the rubble). Invented the diffraction-grating spectroscope and used it to catalogue the dark absorption lines in the solar spectrum (Fraunhofer lines, still named for him) — lines that Kirchhoff would later identify as element fingerprints, opening astrophysics as a discipline. Died of tuberculosis at 39 (a bad year for 39-year-old optics giants).
— §08.1 speed-of-light experimental confirmation + §09 optical-dispersion: Hippolyte Fizeau (1819–1896). French physicist, medical student who switched to physics after a stutter ended his career ambitions in medicine. 1849 measured the speed of light on a Paris-to-Montmartre toothed-wheel setup — the first terrestrial c measurement not requiring astronomical observation. Independent of Foucault (who used a rotating-mirror variation shortly after). Fizeau–Foucault rivalry is a classic French scientific feud.
